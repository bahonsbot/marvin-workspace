# Case Study 08: FTX Collapse (November 2022)

## Event summary
On **November 2022**, FTX — one of the largest cryptocurrency exchanges — collapsed in a matter of days after revelations that customer funds were misused. The scandal led to Sam Bankman-Fried's conviction on fraud charges and exposed systemic risks in the crypto industry.

## Timeline (early signal → news → market reaction)
- **2022 (early signal):** Concerns about Alameda Research (FTX's sister trading firm)
- **June 2022:** CoinDesk reports Alameda has $14.6B in assets, much is FTT token
- **Nov 2, 2022:** CoinDesk investigative report: Alameda balance sheet exposed
- **Nov 6, 2022:** Binance CEO CZ tweets about FTX exposure; announces Binance will sell FTT
- **Nov 7, 2022:** $500M+ FTT token sale attempt; customers begin withdrawing
- **Nov 8, 2022:** Withdrawals spike; FTX pauses withdrawals
- **Nov 9, 2022:** Deal with Binance collapses
- **Nov 11, 2022:** FTX files for Chapter 11 bankruptcy
- **Nov 2022:** SBF arrested in Bahamas; later extradited to US

## Time lag estimate
- From balance sheet concerns (June) to collapse: **~5 months**
- From investigative report (Nov 2) to bankruptcy: **9 days**
- From Binance tweet to bankruptcy: **~3 days**

## Market outcome
- FTT token: -90%+
- Bitcoin: dropped ~15% during crisis (~$16K)
- Crypto winter deepened: 2022 was already brutal year
- Contagion: BlockFi (bankrupted), Genesis (struggled)
- SBF convicted: Nov 2023 — 25 years prison

## Early signal sources (that a system could have monitored)
- Exchange on-chain data (wallet movements)
- Alameda balance sheet / token holdings
- CoinDesk / investigative journalism
- Crypto Twitter (CZ's tweets moved markets)
- Regulatory announcements (SEC investigations)
- Stablecoin de-pegging signals
- Exchange withdrawal patterns

## Pattern lessons
1. **Centralized exchange risk** — "Not your keys, not your crypto"
2. **Contagion is fast** — Binance tweet triggered bank run in hours
3. **Regulatory vacuum** — SEC/CFTC warnings existed but no enforcement
4. **Transparency is rare** — balance sheets were opaque
