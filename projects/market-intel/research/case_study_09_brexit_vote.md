# Case Study 09: Brexit Vote (June 2016)

## Event summary
On **June 23, 2016**, the United Kingdom voted to leave the European Union in a historic referendum. The "Leave" win shocked markets, causing the British pound to crash to a 31-year low and triggering global market volatility.

## Timeline (early signal → news → market reaction)
- **2015-2016 (early signal):** Polls showed close race; Bookmakers had Remain as favorite
- **June 22, 2016:** Markets jittery; pound stable
- **June 23, 2016 (voting day):** Polls close at 10pm BST
- **June 24, 2016 (results):** Leave wins 51.9% vs 48.1%
- **Immediate aftermath:**
  - Pound crashes **8% vs USD** (biggest one-day drop since 1992 Black Wednesday)
  - S&P 500: -3.6%
  - FTSE 250: -12%
  - European banks: -20%+
- **Post-referendum:** PM Cameron resigns; Boris Johnson becomes PM; Brexit negotiations begin

## Time lag estimate
- From poll tightening (weeks before) to vote: **weeks**
- From vote result (10pm) to market open: **hours**
- From result to peak volatility: **same day / next trading session**

## Market outcome
- GBP/USD: $1.50 → $1.32 (-8%) — 31-year low
- FTSE 250: -12% (intra-day)
- European banks: -20%+
- S&P 500: -3.6% (opened down)
- Volatility (VIX): spiked but quickly contained

## Early signal sources (that a system could have monitored)
- Opinion polls (YouGov, ICM, Survation)
- Betting odds (Betfair, PredictIt) — often more accurate than polls
- Currency implied volatility
- CDS spreads on UK banks
- EU referendum campaign news / debates
- Social media sentiment (Twitter/X)

## Pattern lessons
1. **Polling miss = market surprise** — polls underestimated Leave support
2. **Political events can shock markets** — "black swan" vote
3. **Currency moves faster than equities** — GBP dropped 8% in hours
4. **Contagion was contained** — no systemic crisis despite sharp moves
