# Trading Cross-Sector Expansion Research

Date: 2026-03-23
Status: staged research memo
Purpose: extend the value-chain trading framework beyond AI into the next sectors Philippe flagged as strategically important.

## Scope
Target sectors:
1. broader technology stacks
2. mining and rare earths
3. industrial automation
4. energy infrastructure
5. defense supply chains
6. healthcare technology and equipment

---

## Executive recommendation
Expand in **separate staged waves**, not all at once.

Recommended order:
1. **Mining and rare earths**
2. **Energy infrastructure**
3. **Industrial automation**
4. **Defense supply chains**
5. **Broader technology stacks**
6. **Healthcare technology and equipment**

### Why this order
- mining/rare earths and energy infrastructure are the most natural next fit for the current bottleneck/value-chain logic
- industrial automation then becomes a strong follow-on because it shares supplier-chain and category-2 dynamics
- defense supply chains are valuable but more politically/noise-sensitive
- broader technology stacks are useful, but very broad and easy to bloat if not sequenced carefully
- healthcare technology/equipment is valuable but has the most sector-specific nuance and regulatory complexity

---

## 1. Broader technology stacks

### Why it matters
This is the catchment area around AI without collapsing everything into semis.
Useful chains include:
- networking
- cybersecurity
- cloud data infrastructure
- observability / search / data plumbing
- enterprise software with real workflow lock-in

### Best value-chain lenses
- infrastructure vs app layer
- platform owner vs feature wrapper
- secure-by-design / compliance moats
- switching-cost moats
- data gravity and ecosystem lock-in

### Bottlenecks / hidden enablers
- networking gear and interconnects
- identity and security infrastructure
- database / streaming / retrieval layers
- observability and telemetry stack
- cloud cost management / optimization

### Strong candidate archetypes
- platform owners
- network/fabric enablers
- data-layer suppliers
- security vendors with workflow lock-in

### Fragile archetypes
- AI wrappers
- low-differentiation app software
- high-SBC names with weak cash flow
- feature companies exposed to platform bundling

### Why not first
It is strategically important, but broad enough to sprawl unless the more chokepoint-heavy sectors are modeled first.

## 2. Mining and rare earths

### Why it should be first
This is the cleanest next expansion of the current model.
It has:
- chokepoints
- refining bottlenecks
- geopolitical concentration
- category-2 hidden enablers
- strong supplier-chain logic
- natural long/short and pair-trade structure

### Best value-chain layers
- upstream extraction
- midstream refining / separation
- magnet materials / processing
- downstream industrial users
- recycling / recovery

### Key bottlenecks
- separation and processing capacity
- Chinese refining dominance
- permitting timelines
- concentration in magnet production
- geopolitical/export-control risk

### Category-2 / hidden-enabler logic
The real edge is often not the obvious miner.
It is:
- the processor
- the separator
- the refiner
- the magnet maker
- the equipment provider

### Strong candidate archetypes
- processors/refiners with strategic scarcity
- magnet-material leaders
- diversified critical-minerals suppliers
- equipment or chemical process enablers

### Fragile archetypes
- single-asset speculative miners
- names with poor financing flexibility
- downstream industrial users exposed to material squeeze

### Why this sector matches the framework so well
It behaves like AI infrastructure in one important way:
- the economic power is often in the constrained layer, not the loudest narrative layer

### Implementation note
This should become the **first non-AI cross-sector expansion wave** in the tagger and operator board.

## 3. Industrial automation

### Why it matters
Industrial automation is full of preferred-supplier logic, hidden enablers, and workflow lock-in.
It also overlaps nicely with:
- robotics
- controls
- motion systems
- sensors
- machine vision
- industrial software

### Best value-chain layers
- controllers / PLCs
- drives / motion control
- sensors / machine vision
- robotics hardware
- industrial software / MES / digital twin
- system integrators

### Bottlenecks / hidden enablers
- specialized controls vendors
- machine vision / sensing providers
- servo / motion components
- software layers tied into installed industrial bases

### Strong candidate archetypes
- preferred automation vendors
- software + hardware combo suppliers
- niche component leaders with high switching costs

### Fragile archetypes
- low-margin integrators
- commoditized downstream assemblers
- cyclical names without software/service moat

### Why it should be early
It is a good third stage because it shares many framework properties with AI infra and mining:
- supplier chains
- hidden category-2 names
- preferred vendor logic
- high-margin innovation flywheel for leaders

## 4. Energy infrastructure

### Why it matters
This is both a standalone sector and an enabling layer for AI, industrial buildout, electrification, and geopolitical risk.

### Best value-chain layers
- grid equipment
- transformers / switchgear
- generation equipment
- transmission / distribution
- pipelines / storage
- power management
- cooling / electrical infrastructure

### Bottlenecks
- transformer shortages
- grid interconnection delays
- turbine availability
- power equipment lead times
- permitting and transmission bottlenecks

### Strong candidate archetypes
- grid equipment leaders
- power management specialists
- cooling / electrical infrastructure providers
- pipeline/storage infrastructure with strategic position

### Fragile archetypes
- lower-margin downstream users exposed to energy cost spikes
- project-dependent names with financing sensitivity
- broad cyclicals without bottleneck advantage

### Why it is near the front of the queue
It is directly useful to both:
- AI/datacenter buildout logic
- broader macro and infrastructure positioning

This could arguably be stage 2 or stage 1B alongside rare earths.

---

## 5. Defense supply chains

### Why it matters
This sector has:
- long procurement cycles
- supplier concentration
- mission-critical preferred vendors
- electronics / propulsion / munitions bottlenecks
- geopolitical event sensitivity

### Best value-chain layers
- primes
- propulsion
- sensors / radar / electronics
- munitions / energetics
- shipbuilding / aerospace subsystems
- logistics / sustainment

### Bottlenecks
- munitions production capacity
- electronics and guidance components
- propulsion systems
- specialist machining / castings / energetics

### Strong candidate archetypes
- mission-critical subsystem suppliers
- sustainment / electronics leaders
- preferred vendors with hard replacement difficulty

### Fragile archetypes
- lower-tier contractors without protected niche
- politically exposed hype names without durable procurement edge

### Why not earlier
Very valuable, but noisier and more event-driven than mining or industrial automation.
Needs careful signal design so it does not become geopolitics theater.

---

## 6. Healthcare technology and equipment

### Why it matters
This is a good fit for the framework because it also has:
- preferred supplier logic
- installed-base moats
- high switching costs
- innovation and workflow lock-in
- hidden category-2 suppliers

### Best value-chain layers
- diagnostics
- imaging
- surgical robotics
- monitoring
- medtech components
- hospital IT / workflow systems
- consumables tied to installed base

### Bottlenecks
- reimbursement/regulatory timing
- installed-base switching costs
- consumables pull-through
- supply concentration in specialized components

### Strong candidate archetypes
- platform device leaders
- installed-base medtech names
- component/consumable suppliers tied to durable workflows

### Fragile archetypes
- capital equipment names without moat
- overhyped device stories
- companies exposed to hospital budget tightening without recurring pull-through

### Why later
This sector is good, but more domain-specific and less immediately aligned with the current infrastructure-heavy model than the others.

## Most important modeling rule
Do not treat each sector as a flat watchlist.
For each sector, define:
1. value-chain layers
2. bottleneck types
3. category-2 hidden enablers
4. strong vs fragile archetypes
5. operator pools for pair-style research

That preserves the framework discipline instead of degenerating into generic sector coverage.

---

## Recommended next action
Start with **Stage 1: mining and rare earths** as the next concrete implementation wave.

Why:
- strongest fit to the current architecture
- easiest next chain to encode honestly
- high bottleneck and category-2 relevance
- naturally extends beyond AI while still matching the same research logic
